## ACT 2: THE OFFERINGS (5:30 - 10:30)

### Scene 07 — Cain's Offering
**Duration:** 55 seconds
**AI Image Prompt:** Cain standing before a rough stone altar, placing a casual pile of fruits and vegetables on it. His expression is indifferent, almost bored. The offering looks careless — some produce tossed on the stones. Dark moody atmosphere, muted colors. Thin wisp of smoke barely rising. Cinematic, biblical art, hyper-realistic.
**On-Screen Text:** *""*
**Scripture:** *"Genesis 4:3 — In the course of time Cain brought some of the fruits of the soil as an offering to the Lord."*

**Narration:**
Genesis 4:3 says, "In the course of time Cain brought some of the fruits of the soil as an offering to the Lord." Pay close attention to the language here. "Some of the fruits." Not the first. Not the best. Just... some. The Hebrew phrasing suggests this was ordinary, unremarkable produce. Cain brought what was convenient. What cost him nothing extra. His offering was technically correct — he brought something. But it lacked something essential. It lacked reverence. It lacked sacrifice. It lacked the heart behind the gift. Cain went through the motions. He checked the box. But his heart was not in it. And God sees the heart.

**On-Screen Text:** *""*

But Abel — Abel brought something different. Genesis 4:4 says, "Abel also brought an offering — fat portions from some of the firstborn of his flock." Every word here matters. He brought the firstborn — not the leftovers, but the first and the best. He brought the fat portions — in ancient culture, the fat was considered the richest, most valuable part of the animal. Abel did not give God what was convenient. He gave God his best. His most valuable. His first. This is the heart of true worship. It costs something. It requires sacrifice. Abel understood something that Cain did not — that when you come before God, you do not bring Him your leftovers. You bring Him your first and your finest.

**On-Screen Text:** *""*

**Narration:**
And then came the moment that changed everything. "The Lord looked with favor on Abel and his offering, but on Cain and his offering he did not look with favor." God accepted Abel's sacrifice and rejected Cain's. Now — why? Was it because one was an animal and the other was fruit? Some scholars believe so, pointing to the blood sacrifice as a foreshadow of Christ. But the deeper truth is found in Hebrews 11:4, which tells us, "By faith Abel brought God a better offering than Cain did." It was not just about what they brought. It was about the faith behind it. Abel gave with a heart of worship, trust, and surrender. Cain gave out of obligation. And God, who looks not at the outward appearance but at the heart, saw the difference. This is a warning that echoes through all of Scripture — God is not impressed by religious performance. He wants your heart.
